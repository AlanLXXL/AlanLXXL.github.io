## Watching Bendou: On Systems, Outliers, and the Illusion of Solving

I recently watched the latest episode from *Movie Hurricane* (《影视飓风》) — a deep interview with **Bendou (笨豆)**, a sixteen-year-old high school student whose self-made documentaries have already crossed a billion views. She started making videos in fourth grade, and by now has traveled alone to India, Sri Lanka, and Mongolia to film humanistic and nature documentaries.

Watching her speak, I found I couldn't look away — not because of what she had done, but because of how calmly clear-headed she was about it.

[Original video on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dRYtB9Qe3Jg)

---

### A Workflow Carved Out of Constraints

What struck me first was how *unprofessional* her process is — in the best possible sense.

Because her school does not allow electronic devices, she does her "editing" on paper during free periods, sketching timelines and themes by hand, then mentally pre-rendering the cut in her head. The actual editing happens on a single iPad. Long-format projects make the iPad lag, so she has a small ritual: before opening the app, she "wishes" to the device, asking it not to crash. To keep up with high school, she edits late into the night until she falls asleep without realizing.

This is the opposite of how we usually picture "good work." No studio, no team, no ergonomic setup. Just a kid with a tablet, a script on paper, and the patience to keep going.

### Sincerity as Method

People assumed her polished documentaries had to come from a hidden professional team. The truth is that there isn't one — at least not in the conventional sense.

When she filmed in Mongolia, she simply set a small camera on a table and asked questions face-to-face: no complicated rig, no production crew, just direct curiosity. The people behind the scenes are her peers — a junior-high music friend she found online who scored the soundtrack, classmates who drew illustrations in exchange for a few cups of milk tea.

Sincerity, it turns out, is not a fallback for when you don't have resources. It is the method itself.

### A Sixteen-Year-Old's Honesty About Privilege

The part I respected most came when the interviewer asked about a recurring comment under her videos — that only a kid with money and family resources could make work like hers.

She did not flinch. She agreed, calmly. Before turning eighteen, the financial backing of her family, including paying for her trips abroad, has been a real and decisive factor. She does not pretend otherwise.

She also admitted that as her audience grew, the "creator" identity started sharing space with the "operator" identity, and the original purity of her enthusiasm has thinned a little. If one day she stops making videos, she would happily become a custom-route tour guide — because that, too, is a way of seeing how different people live.

In her old notebook, the director found a sentence she had written at age ten, in 2021: *"I want to make better videos."* The pinyin was wrong. The seed was not.

---

### My Thoughts: On Evaluation Systems

Watching this, I kept circling back to one question: the evaluation systems we live inside.

The Gaokao gets criticized endlessly, but it also does something genuinely valuable — it protects the majority. It is a relatively fair game, where a number on a piece of paper roughly decides where you go. Compare that to elite American admissions, where "storytelling," passion projects, and curated extracurriculars are quietly underwritten by family wealth, networks, and access. A score, for all its flatness, is the closest thing most people get to a level playing field.

But a score-based system is brutal to outliers. Love for a craft, anti-fragility, the patience to draft a script on paper for months because you don't own a laptop — none of that fits inside a rubric. People like Bendou, or Danfei Xu (from the *Why Not TV* cast), survive *despite* the system, not because of it. And most of us are not Bendou. Most of us first become victims of the evaluation system, and then — once we make it through — turn around and enforce the same rules on whoever comes next.

### Two Questions I Cannot Resolve

**First.** The Gaokao gives most of us relative fairness, yes. But does the same system also harden a hierarchy of professions — a quiet social agreement about which jobs are noble and which are beneath us? Lu Xun's *Kong Yiji* still reads like a description of right now: the long scholar's gown you cannot bring yourself to take off, even when it leaves you starving.

**Second.** The freedom of British and American education — choose your own path, follow your passion — is partly funded by the safety net of a mature, wealthy society. When that society starts pulling against itself, when economic pressure tightens, is that freedom still real, or is it a surface prosperity that only works in good weather?

I don't have answers to either. I'm not sure anyone does.

---

### Conclusion: The Illusion of Solving

There are many things that cannot be solved. Which means, strangely, that "solving the problem" is itself an illusion.

Gaokao versus the Western system isn't a debate with a winner. Bendou's privilege and Bendou's talent aren't in competition; they're both true at once. Outliers and rules cannot be reconciled by a better rubric. I keep wanting to look at things from a balanced place — not because balance is the correct answer, but because the craving for a clean answer is usually where the distortion begins.

> **缘起性空。**  
> **凡一切相，皆是虚妄。既见诸相非相，即见如来。**
>
> *All things arise from conditions and are empty of fixed essence.*  
> *All forms are illusions; only when you see that forms are not forms do you see the truth.*

A sixteen-year-old with an iPad reminded me of this, in her own way.

## 中文版：看笨豆 — 评价体系、outlier 与「解决」的幻觉

最近看了一期《影视飓风》的视频——他们采访了一位叫**笨豆**的16岁高中生创作者，全网播放量已经破亿。她从小学四年级就开始做视频，常常独自一人前往印度、斯里兰卡、蒙古国拍摄人文与自然纪录片。

看完之后我久久没办法移开。不仅是因为她做了什么，更是因为她说话时那种平静的清醒。

[原视频链接](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dRYtB9Qe3Jg)

---

### 一、在限制里磨出来的工作流

最让我意外的是她的"不专业"——是最好意义上的那种不专业。

因为学校不允许带电子设备，她会在课余的空闲时间里用纸把事件的时间线和主题写下来，然后在脑海里把整个片子提前"渲染"一遍。真正的剪辑只发生在一台 iPad 上。长视频很容易卡，所以她有一个开机仪式——打开剪辑软件之前对 iPad 许个愿，让它别崩。为了兼顾高中学业，她常常剪到不知不觉就睡着了。

这跟我们被反复告知的"好作品如何诞生"完全相反。没有工作室，没有团队，没有人体工学桌椅。只有一个小孩、一台 iPad、一张纸上的脚本，和把这件事坚持下去的耐心。

### 二、真诚不是退路，是方法

外界曾经默认这种成熟的纪录片背后必然有一支专业团队。事实却出人意料：根本没有所谓的团队。

在蒙古国拍摄时，她就是简单地把一台相机架在桌上，面对面用最直接的提问去和当地人交流。没有复杂的设备阵仗，只有一份纯粹的好奇。幕后的帮手是她身边的同龄人——一位还在初二的网友帮她编曲，几个同学用几杯奶茶换来了插画。

真诚原来不是"没有资源时的备选方案"，它本身就是方法。

### 三、十六岁的诚实

最让我尊敬的部分，是她面对评论区那句"有钱、有家庭资源才能做这种视频"的质疑时的回答。

她没有反驳，也没有急着辩解。她平静地承认了——18岁之前，家庭的经济支持、对她的信任（包括为她支付出国拍摄的费用），确实是非常关键的支撑。她没有假装这一切是凭空而来的。

她也坦白：随着影响力变大，"创作者"的身份和"运营者"的身份开始挤在一起，最初那份纯粹的热情确实被消耗了一些。如果有一天不拍视频了，她想去做定制路线的导游——因为那同样是在看不同的人怎么活着。

在她的旧本子里，导演翻到了她10岁时（2021年）写下的一句话："我想做更好的视频。"那时候她连拼音都没写对，但那颗种子是对的。

---

### 我的一些想法：关于评价体系

看完之后，我一直在想一件事——我们身处其中的评价体系。

高考被骂了很多年，但它其实做了一件相当珍贵的事：它保护了大多数人。它是一场相对公平的游戏，一张卷子上的分数大致决定了你去哪里。对比美国精英藤校的录取——那种"故事性"的包装、passion project 的塑造、漂亮的课外活动——背后悄悄站着的，是家庭财力、人脉和资源。分数虽然冰冷，却是大部分人能触碰到的最接近"公平"的东西。

但分数体系对 outlier 是残忍的。对一个职业近乎本能的热爱、反脆弱的韧性、用纸代替剪辑软件的耐心——这些都没办法塞进一张评分表里。像笨豆，像 *Why Not TV* 里的 Danfei Xu，他们是在体系之外活下来的，不是因为体系而活下来的。而我们大多数人不是笨豆。大多数人先成为评价体系的受害者，然后——在自己穿过去之后——再回过头来把同一套规则压给下一代。

### 两个我自己解决不了的问题

**第一**：高考给了大部分人相对的公平，没错。但同样的系统是不是也固化了社会对不同职业的高低贵贱？是不是在制造孔乙己的长衫现象——那件你舍不得脱下的长衫，哪怕它已经让你饿肚子。

**第二**：英美教育制度那种"自由"——选你自己的路、追你自己的热情——其实有一部分是建立在一个高度成熟、富裕社会的兜底之上的。当那个社会内部开始相互拉扯，经济压力持续加大时，这种自由还是真的自由，还是只是天气好的时候才成立的表面繁荣？

这两个问题我都没有答案。可能也没有人有。

---

### 结语：解决问题本身是一种幻觉

很多事情其实是无法被"解决"的。这意味着，"解决问题"这件事本身就是一种幻觉。

高考与西方教育体系之间不是一场有胜者的辩论。笨豆的家庭资源和笨豆的天赋不是对立项，它们同时为真。Outlier 和规则之间，也不可能通过一个更好的评分表来调和。我总是想从一个相对平衡的角度去看问题——不是因为"平衡"就是正确答案，而是因为对一个干净答案的渴望，往往就是失真开始的地方。

> **缘起性空。**  
> **凡一切相，皆是虚妄。既见诸相非相，即见如来。**

一个手里只有一台 iPad 的十六岁女孩，用她自己的方式提醒了我这一点。
